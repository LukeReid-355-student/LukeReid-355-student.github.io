Pacific Rhododendron

Rhododendron macrophyllum — Washington State Flower

Overview

The Pacific Rhododendron, or Rhododendron macrophyllum, is a broadleaf evergreen shrub native to the Pacific Northwest. Its name means “rose tree with big leaves,” and it is celebrated as the official state flower of Washington. This plant’s large, pink to rose-purple blossoms brighten coastal and mountain forests in late spring and early summer.

Identification Features

  • Family: Ericaceae (Heath family)
  • Height: Typically 3–15 feet, up to 25 feet in shaded forests
  • Leaves: Thick, leathery, and evergreen — 3–8 inches long
  • Flowers: Bell-shaped clusters of pink to rose-purple blooms
  • Habitat: Moist forests, coastal slopes, and along streams west of the Cascades

Blooming Season & Ecology

The Pacific Rhododendron typically blooms between May and July. Its flowers attract bumblebees, butterflies, and hummingbirds. The plant provides shelter for birds and small animals within its dense evergreen structure.

Conservation & Gardening

This species thrives in well-drained, acidic soils and partial shade. When planting in gardens, avoid disturbing its shallow roots. The Pacific Rhododendron is protected in the wild—do not dig or remove wild plants; instead, purchase from reputable native nurseries.
